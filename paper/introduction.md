# 1. Introduction

The study of dissipative oscillatory media constitutes a cornerstone of non-equilibrium statistical physics and the theory of complex dynamical systems. These systems govern emergent collective phenomena across a wide array of physical domains, ranging from plasma interactions and biological neural networks to localized networks of coupled oscillators. Over the past several decades, two primary frameworks have dominated the theoretical literature of these media: the Kuramoto model for clean phase oscillator networks, and the Complex Ginzburg-Landau Equation (CGLE), which integrates phase and amplitude dynamics within a continuous effective field.

Despite the immense mathematical success of these standard formulations, they generally assume smooth, analytic coupling functions and linear or even-powered energy dissipation envelopes (such as the cubic damping term $|Z|^2Z$ in the standard CGLE). However, these classical formulations face clear conceptual limitations when mapped onto realistic physical systems characterized by hard activation thresholds or non-uniform, spatially heterogeneous energy dissipation governed by material properties or interstitial boundary geometries.

In this context, we introduce a hybrid microscopic framework designated as the Fractionally Damped Spatial Oscillator Network (FDSON). This model aims to explore the collective behaviors that emerge when three unconventional dynamical elements interact: first, a strict threshold coupling mechanism that halts the transmission of dynamical information unless the integrated stimulus exceeds a critical activation value; second, an elastic geometric interaction range that expands and contracts dynamically according to the instantaneous amplitude of the responding oscillator; and third, a non-linear fractional dissipative damping envelope governed by the non-integer exponent $\alpha = 1.5$.

The introduction of the fractional dissipative exponent $A^{1.5}$ serves as the primary dynamical driver in this model. It automatically generates a localized, amplitude-dependent dissipation gradient across individual nodes. This non-uniform dissipation acts as a dynamic geometric sieve, effectively preventing the system from collapsing into global synchronization, while simultaneously insulating the sustained irregular dynamics from trivial decay or extinction.

This research addresses a central question regarding the exact nature of the emergent dynamical object within the FDSON framework, establishing the precise spectral and fractal properties of its asymptotic attractor. Utilizing a rigorous numerical verification protocol based on extensive ensemble statistics and full tangent-space analysis, this work tests classical thermodynamic narratives—such as genuine thermodynamic phase transitions, subcritical bistability, and stable spatial Turing instabilities—and evaluates their structural persistence under threshold constraints and fractional damping.

The remainder of this manuscript is organized as follows: Section 2 outlines the rigorous mathematical formulation of the microscopic model, the numerical integration protocol, and the tangent-space Lyapunov spectrum optimization technique. Section 3 presents the quantitative empirical results verified by standard errors and confidence intervals. Section 4 evaluates the physical mechanisms, underlying dynamics, and open questions, concluding with Section 5, which summarizes the core findings of the project.
